Continuity of Care Across Provinces In Canada: A Case Study

CONTINUING UNIVERSITY PROJECT   |   SEPTEMBER 2, 2025 - DECEMBER 8, 2025

Moving provinces can be an exciting new chapter, but for many people, it also means dealing with (and for some, discovering) messy healthcare rules and bureaucracy. For those on regular medication, transferring prescriptions between provinces can be confusing, stressful, and poorly documented. I chose this project because I saw a real gap: there‚Äôs no centralized, trustworthy, easy-to-follow guide for people moving to British Columbia (BC) from other provinces in Canada, trying to navigate the steps, pharmacies, and different provincial rules. The goal was simple: to make that transition easier and more transparent.

MY PROJECT:

A guided tool that lives in the official BC Health site, a place people already trust when searching for health info. Instead of building a brand-new app (which people might hesitate to download), it will be a supportive, more personalized guide that helps people understand exactly what they need to do to transfer their prescription safely.

Research Process

To avoid basing the project on assumptions, I used a mix of secondary and primary research.

SECONDARY RESEARCH INCLUDED:

  • Reviewing informal sources: online forums where people ask questions like ‚ÄúHow do I transfer my meds to BC?‚Äù
  • Looking at official BC Health sites and resources to understand what‚Äôs already provided, and what‚Äôs missing (competitive analysis)

PRIMARY RESEARCH INCLUDED:

  • Interviewing users who moved to BC from another province and had to transfer medication
  • Interviewing pharmacists and pharmacy assistants, discussing process challenges with pharmacists and pharmacy assistants

After gathering all the research, I discovered common pain points. Unclear timelines, inconsistent pharmacy processes, lack of centralized guidance, uncertainty about what documents or steps are required. These all came together to a few recurring themes: confusion, anxiety, distrust in the different information, and a real need for clarity. These findings directly shaped the design: this tool wouldn‚Äôt try to ‚Äúdo the transfer for them,‚Äù because that‚Äôs unrealistic given all the government red tape and varying provincial rules. Instead, it would guide, support, and clarify.

Research Insights & Their Impact on Design

From research, these key insights came up:

  • ‚ÄúI don‚Äôt know where to start.‚Äù Many people shared that they simply didn‚Äôt know which pharmacy to call or which info they‚Äôd need when moving provinces.
  • Rules vary by province and pharmacy. There‚Äôs no universal standard, which means any ‚Äúone-size-fits-all‚Äù approach will fail.
  • People distrust random apps. When handling prescriptions and healthcare, trust matters. Many would only follow guidance from a respected, recognizable source (like a government site).
  • During a move, stress is high. Moving involves many tasks, adding medical transfers on top can result in missed doses or forgotten steps.

Because of this, I grounded the design around three core values:

The tool becomes a trusted guide that walks users step by step through their situation. No assumptions, no surprises.

Conceptual Development: How the Idea Evolved

Originally, I thought about building a tool that directly connected pharmacies across provinces, maybe even automate parts of the transfer. But when I mapped this vision using this design-framework, the gaps became clear.

I REALIZED:

  • The different rules across provinces is massive, and connecting all systems under one tool would require cooperation on a federal level.
  • Prescription transfers often involve human verification (IDs, health numbers, pharmacy approvals). Automation could cause security and privacy issues, or fail.
  • For a user, the process of downloading a new app introduces unnecessary barriers. Many might avoid it entirely.

I redirected the approach. The new concept: a guided tool embedded in the existing BC Health system. That means no need to download anything, and it carries official legitimacy. The tool walks users through a personalized process based on their previous province, whether they have refills left, and whether they‚Äôre switching pharmacies; delivering a checklist, timelines, and clear next steps.

This transformation made the concept more realistic, but also more helpful. Instead of promising full automation, it promised clarity, support, and a reduction of uncertainty.

Validation: Why This Isn‚Äôt ‚ÄúJust My Idea‚Äù

To validate the concept, I shared the idea with people who had moved to BC and with pharmacy staff. Their feedback confirmed the pain points and the need for a trusted, step-by-step resource.

The Solution: How I Envision the Concept

Because it lives inside an official site, it carries trust. Because it‚Äôs personalized, it feels realistic. Because it doesn‚Äôt promise more than what‚Äôs possible, it stays ethically and technically grounded.

Next Steps

Right now, this project is a strong proof-of-concept. The next phase would be building low to mid-fidelity prototypes and running usability tests with real users to refine the flow. I also plan to work with accessibility guidelines to ensure the tool works for all users.

What I learned: real design isn‚Äôt about what you want to build. It‚Äôs about what you should build, what fits within systems, meets real human needs, and respects constraints. That pivot, from automation to guidance, is what makes this idea honest, realistic, and truly helpful.
